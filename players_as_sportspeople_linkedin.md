# Players as sportspeople in competitive multiplayer video games

One of the strangest shifts in online competitive multiplayer design is how they often treat ordinary players as sportspeople.

It's the way that a lot of games are structured nowadays. You queue up and you're dropped into a temporary, time-boxed server. The match will end, one team will win, the other will lose, and that outcome is logged somewhere as a permanent behavioural record, even if the mode claims to be 'casual' or 'unranked'.

It doesn't matter whether a mode is labelled 'for fun' when the behavioural architecture is still saying:

'Perform, and be judged for your performance.'

From a behavioural design perspective, this is crazy. We've built systems that turn every session into a test of performance, which gets archived and shapes future matchmaking.

A lot of games that are structured this way lack a living pressure valve. The game often isn't designed for players to just pick a character and mess around in a social sandbox with other players who share their intentions. Most of those players are queueing for the front-facing modes or they're in private sandboxes.

Even if a player tries to put together one of these private sandboxes - a custom game - it often still carries the affordances of a regular game, and it's functionally solitary unless you make the game public and hope people join, who may or may not share your intentions.

There's a shortage of games that contain persistent, front-facing social servers that are prioritised like 'Unranked' and 'Ranked' modes are. The public sandboxes, the 'join and leave whenever, stay as long as you like' environments appear to be a relic of the past.

So what happens when there's no third choice, the ability to just 'exist' inside a system, to warm up, to decompress, to experiment, or to be bad at the game? 

You get churn, anxiety, burnout, and a game that feels like a job.

That leaves you with a binary choice. Play and be evaluated, or leave.

These games are designed to hit KPIs, and that isn't compatible with how humans really work.
