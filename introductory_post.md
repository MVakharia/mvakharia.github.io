# Introductory post

Over the past seven months I’ve been diving deeper into the problems and issues I’ve always thought about, and I’ve realised that my life’s centre of gravity is behavioural design: the way environments and systems shape behaviour. 

I put it together only recently, once I started examining my thought patterns and attempting to look at the throughline. Every issue I’m drawn to and every problem I want to solve can be explored through the lens of behavioural design.

We’re all products of environments that shape behaviour. If your family, friends, schools, workplaces, or even your home country had been different, you would be different. 

Our behaviour is shaped by our life experiences, and experiences can be designed or altered.

One thing that led me to the throughline is the way I think as a field service engineer. Yes, I’m there to fix or upgrade equipment, but the part of the work that has always interested me the most is the casual ethnography I do on site:

- Listening to staff talk about their experiences with the IT equipment they rely on.
- Speaking with my managers about the cost of maintaining legacy systems versus rolling out new ones.
- Seeing the relief on the faces of shop‑floor staff when an engineer arrives, and their dismay when they discover that an engineer is unable to carry out the fix, not knowing that the engineer is untrained, running on fumes, or deployed to hit SLAs rather than to repair anything.

These experiences have taught me what happens when systems aren’t designed for the way humans really behave.

And it’s not just field service. I see the same patterns everywhere:

- Games that expect competence without scaffolding
- Technology that assumes everybody is connected, tech-savvy, and that the tech is error-free
- Systems that punish people for not already knowing how they work

They’re all failures of design. We often put the blame onto ourselves for not having an attention span, motivation, or willpower, but we shouldn’t have to rely on these to get through life. If we do, there’s something fundamentally wrong with the system or with the environment, which means that it needs to change.

Right now I’m building a research programme around behavioural design, and I’m looking for a place where I can use these skills and insights to solve real problems that affect real people.

I don’t yet have commercial experience in behavioural design, UX research, or service design; my value comes from a systems-thinking mind and the lens I can offer.
