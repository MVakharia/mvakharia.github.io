# The monetisation of job ads

We already know that a lot of job advertisements are 'ghost jobs': roles posted to harvest data. But there's something else going on.

A lot of online content is monetised; it's ad supported, generates clicks, impressions, repeat visits, or is supported by paying subscribers. That's the economic engine of the modern internet.

We treat job boards as neutral infrastructure, a kind of public utility for hiring, but in reality, a job board is just another monetisable content platform.

Job ads are content on these platforms, which means that no matter the purpose of a job ad, it's generating value as long as it's up.

Inactive or fake job posts inflate the perceived size of the job market, they increase ad impressions, and they make the board look 'busy'. None of these things require the job to be real or for it to still exist. The job just has to be visible, and clickable.

This is a classic example of misaligned incentives: job seekers want jobs, employers want reach, and job boards want volume. Employers will pay job boards for that level of reach, and job boards have every incentive to keep any kind of listing alive, regardless of its purpose.

It's just what happens when the system isn't built around job seekers' needs.
