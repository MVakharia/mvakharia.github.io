# Habit formation in easy mode, part 2

In a previous post I argued that lower game difficulty levels often instill the wrong habits in players. They do this by scaling horizontally: making you aware of your whole kit while giving you no reason to use much of it. 

Later, the same system demands the kind of verticality you haven't built because you didn't build the foundations. 

There's another layer to this problem: the kind of learning produced by a game is directly affected by how the game is made easier. 

There are two approaches: the forgiving version and the shortcut version. 

1. Making the game harder to lose. 

This is the forgiving version of 'easy'. Giving the player more health, more recovery, more of a buffer. It keeps the problem structure intact, so that players still need to use their full toolkit to solve the problem; they just get way more breathing room to figure it out. 

This approach produces vertical scaling: skills deepen, behaviours integrate, and the mental model grows upwards.

2. Making the game easier to win.

This is the shortcut version of 'easy'. The game becomes trivial, mechanics become optional, and as a result they fall out of use. The player succeeds with less, so they learn less.

This produces horizontal scaling: broad exposure to a toolkit without depth, and recognition of skills without fluency. 

Both approaches feel like 'easy', but they shape completely different players. One approach adds more rungs to the ladder, and the other approach removes them until you don't need to climb the ladder any more. 

Only one of these approaches will adequately prepare you for a game that expects depth in the future. 

The broader lesson is that if you want players to learn deeply, increase your game's forgiveness and reduce shortcuts. Forgiveness expands the space for true mastery, while shortcuts collapse it.

Give your players space to solve a problem, rather than reducing the scope of the problem until it becomes trivial.
