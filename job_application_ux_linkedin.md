# Job application UX

The conversation around job application UX has already happened: I want to talk about something else.

We know that a lot of forms feel like punishment: they attempt to take your CV apart to prefill a bunch of fields, or they make you retype your CV, or they feel like text-based one-way interviews.

This raises the question: what kind of applicant are you attracting when the application process feels bad?

There's this misconception that a painful hiring funnel filters for commitment, for 'the people that truly want the job', but that's not the kind of applicant you're really getting.

What you're getting is an applicant with a high tolerance for pointless friction, who not only doesn't mind having their time and energy wasted, but is willing to endure it.

Meanwhile, the candidates you actually want are the ones who will leave as soon as the process becomes a chore.

The behavioural sweet spot looks more like this:

- Name
- Email
- Phone number (maybe)
- CV and other attachments
- Up to five closed-ended questions: the kind that can be answered with a checkbox, a radio button, or a dropdown.

That's it. Everything else can be validated in the interview.

If you want better applicants, build a better funnel. Test the hiring tools you invest in. Look at them from the perspective of an applicant.

If you want an applicant who cares about your company, show that you care about their time.
