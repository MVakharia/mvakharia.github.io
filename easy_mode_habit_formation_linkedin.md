# Habit formation on easy difficulty modes

There's a subtle design problem in some games: lower difficulty settings or levels often unintentionally train players out of the behaviours expected by higher levels. Players aren't avoiding learning, it's just the way the system shapes player habits.

If a challenge doesn't require a mechanic, we'll be less inclined to use it, and if it drops out of the loop, we forget it exists. 

You'll see this in action when a game's lower levels let you ignore a chunk of the toolkit. Abilities with long cooldowns, secondary systems, tactical items, anything that isn't strictly necessary is likely to be forgotten.

Players will forget these mechanics and then step up a difficulty level or progress through the game and suddenly, they'll feel punished for not using tools that the game provided them, but never encouraged them to practice.

This is a failure of design that players misattribute to themselves. When lower levels don't reinforce the behaviours that the game will demand later, players build a mental model that hasn't yet scaled horizontally, rather than one that can naturally scale vertically.

The model is optimised for the current situation and it doesn't take into account future situations.

This is a design issue that can be fixed by designing levels to force engagement with mechanics that would be otherwise neglected, or by implementing modifiers that spotlight them.

The broader lesson here is bigger than games: if an environment makes essential behaviours feel like they aren't, people are less inclined to develop them. So when a game's difficulty spikes, it feels like a betrayal. 

Good difficulty design isn't just about knowing when and how to implement horizontal and vertical difficulty; it's also about making the right behaviours unavoidable.
